Caregiver & Community Resources | Las Vegas, NV
Physical Care

Safe lifting & transfers

Protect your body
  • Always bend at the knees, never at the waist
  • Keep your loved one close to your body during transfers
  • Use a gait belt — never lift by arms or clothing
  • Ask your PT to teach transfer techniques specific to your situation
  • Use a transfer board or slide sheet for heavier transfers

Protect your sleep

A safety issue, not a luxury
  • Sleep deprivation impairs judgment, patience, and physical ability
  • Ask a family member or respite worker to cover one night a week
  • Consider a medical alert device so you can rest between needs
  • Talk to your doctor if you are consistently getting under 6 hours

Keep your own health appointments

Your health matters too
  • Caregivers frequently delay or skip their own medical care
  • Schedule primary care, dental, and specialist visits in advance
  • Tell your doctor you are a full-time caregiver — it is clinically relevant
  • Arrange coverage so you can attend without guilt or interruption

Skin & pressure sore prevention

Critical for wheelchair users
  • Reposition every 15–30 min in a wheelchair, every 2 hours in bed
  • Keep skin clean and dry — moisture causes breakdown
  • Inspect skin daily over bony areas: tailbone, heels, hips, shoulders
  • Use a pressure-relief cushion — ask your PT which type is right
  • Report any redness, blistering, or open skin to your care team immediately
Emotional Health

What you may be feeling

You are not alone in this
  • Grief — mourning who your loved one was, even while they are still here
  • Guilt — feeling bad for needing breaks or feeling frustrated
  • Resentment — normal and human; does not mean you love them less
  • Isolation — losing your routines and social life to caregiving
  • Fear — about the future, finances, or what happens if you cannot continue

Simple daily coping strategies

Evidence-based tools
  • Box breathing: Inhale 4 sec, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat 3–4 times
  • Name one small win daily — write it down. It reframes perspective over time
  • Protect at least one small pleasure each day: coffee, a walk, a phone call
  • Be specific when asking for help — "Can you bring dinner Thursday?" works better than general requests
  • Limit screens and news in the hour before sleep
Recognizing & Preventing Burnout
Caregiver burnout is common — and treatable

Warning Signs

  • Constant exhaustion that sleep does not fix
  • Feeling hopeless, trapped, or resentful most days
  • Getting sick more often than usual
  • Withdrawing from friends and activities
  • Increased reliance on alcohol or other substances
  • Snapping at your loved one or other family members
  • Feeling like nothing you do is ever enough

What Actually Helps

  • Scheduled respite, even a few hours per week
  • Joining a caregiver support group, in person or online
  • Seeing a therapist briefly — many accept insurance
  • Being honest with your care team about how you are doing
  • Defining clearly what you can and cannot take on
  • Taking at least one full day off per month
  • Calling Nevada 2-1-1 to find local respite services
Practical Questions
Use a weekly pill organizer and set phone alarms labeled with the medication name. Keep an up-to-date list — name, dose, time, and prescribing doctor — in your wallet and on the fridge. Bring it to every appointment. Apps like Medisafe send reminders and flag interactions.
Emotional and behavioral changes are common after stroke due to brain injury — they are not deliberate.
  • Stay calm; speak slowly in short, clear sentences
  • Reduce background noise during difficult moments
  • Pseudobulbar affect — uncontrollable laughing or crying — affects up to 20% of stroke survivors and is treatable. Ask a doctor
  • Consistent daily routines reduce anxiety and outbursts significantly
Keep one binder or folder containing: diagnosis summary, medication list, insurance cards, advance directives, and the PT home exercise program. Take notes at every appointment. Maintain a brief daily log of mood, sleep, bowel/bladder, and any falls. Request patient portal access for test results and visit notes.
  • Nevada Medicaid HCBS Waiver — may cover attendant care, respite, home modifications, and meals for physically disabled adults
  • ADSD Respite Vouchers — quarterly vouchers for in-home respite through Nevada DHHS
  • FreedomCare — Medicaid-enrolled Nevadans may qualify to be paid to care for a family member
  • VA Aid & Attendance — additional pension for veterans needing in-home care
  • Nevada 2-1-1 — call 211 for emergency financial, utility, and housing assistance
This guide is for general education only. It does not replace individualized medical, legal, or financial advice. Always consult your care team with specific questions.

Local Las Vegas Organizations

Nevada Care Connection

State — Free

Resource navigators help caregivers and people with disabilities connect with transportation, home care, respite, meals, and other local services — one-on-one, at no cost.

Phone: Dial 2-1-1 to connect
Web: nevadacareconnection.org
Hours: Mon–Thu 9 AM–5 PM, Fri 9 AM–4 PM

Nevada Senior Services

Local — Sliding Scale

Non-medical home care, respite care, caregiver training, adult day programs, and home safety modifications for seniors and adults with disabilities in Clark County.

Web: nevadaseniorservices.org
Note: Adult day recreation centers available with low caregiver-to-client ratios

Clark County Senior Services

Local — Multiple Services

County-level services including health programs, legal assistance, housing support, and senior center programming for older adults and people with disabilities.

Phone: 702-455-7051
Web: clarkcountynv.gov

Nevada's primary state agency for disability and aging. Connects families with Medicaid waivers, respite vouchers, adult protective services, and long-term care planning.

Phone: 702-486-3545 (Las Vegas)

988

Suicide & Crisis Lifeline — call or text 988

Available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. For patients, caregivers, or anyone in emotional crisis. You do not have to be suicidal to call — overwhelm and hopelessness count too.
